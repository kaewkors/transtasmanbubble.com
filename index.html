Introduction

Canberra gets cold. I know. I rode my bike to work in Woden from Barton around Lake Burley Griffin for about 5 years. Several years ago I decided to follow a girl and move to Wellington and start a new chapter in the New Zealand Public Service. Wellington is windy. I know that too. But it is also beautiful, with a friendly people and a diverse art and culture scene with many great restaurants, bars and cafes. The population of both Canberra and Wellington is roughly equivalent with lots of new build apartments being built in the last twelve months in Wellington to fulfil a historical shortage of housing.

Aussie and Kiwi

Wellington and Canberra

The New Zealand Public Service is comparable to the Federal Australian Public Service in many ways. The origins and conventions strongly reflect the British heritage as in Canberra however in Wellington less emphasis has been given to a so called 'Washminster' blend of Washington and London system of Parliament and Governance than was visible in Canberra. The competitive tensions of the Government and Opposition are still present, afterall that is the purpose of our Parliamentary structures and system. However to date the New Zealand system and parties have been fairly successful in keeping the toxic identity politics and dog whistle nature of political speeches and statement, oppose and propose.

I was surprised that when I moved here (still the same) Australian Citizens do not need a visa, work permit or any formal document before they arrive in New Zealand, there is also no requirement to be a New Zealand citizen to work in 99% of Government agency roles in Wellington. They also operate a centralised recruitment front door like the one for the APS. https://jobs.govt.nz/

Closeness of the Government agencies: they essentially all occupy locations within about 2km of each other in the Wellington CBD, physically and structurally is an asset for collaboration and sharing resources and knowledge. https://ssc.govt.nz/our-work/state-sector-organisations

The main difference though falls down to the fact that New Zealand does not have State or Provincial level Governments. They have local authorities and national level Government agencies. This includes Health, Education, Social Services. As well as the traditionally federal authority realms of Defence, Immigration, Foreign Affairs etc. This means that the impact of policy interventions can be seen with a higher degree of clarity than in a federalism system of Government

New Zealand Parliament is also Unicameral meaning that it doesn't not have two chambers of Parliament, only one. However a robust Parliamentary committee system and independent auditor function keeps the system in balance. This means that law passage is often a far more agile and speedy process when it needs to be, compared to Canberra.
